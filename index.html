Chapter One

The Story

I'm Paul Adcock. I'm a software engineer — self-taught, relentless, and proud of every line of code I've ever shipped. I worked at Syncreon, which was acquired by DP World — one of the largest logistics and supply chain companies on the planet. My role was in technology supporting automotive logistics and sequencing operations.

I showed up. I delivered. I built systems that actually worked in a high-pressure, real-world environment where mistakes cost money and delays cost contracts. I cared about the work more than I was probably supposed to.

And then DP World let me go.

You can fire the person, but you can't fire what they built.

No explanation that made sense. No recognition of what was delivered. Just gone. That's the reality of how Syncreon and DP World treated an engineer who put everything into the work.

I won't fabricate. I won't name individuals. I don't need to. The truth — that a company worth billions discarded a dedicated engineer for reasons that had nothing to do with the quality of work — is damning enough on its own.

But here's what they didn't count on: I'm a builder. You can take the desk, the badge, the title. You can't take what's in my head, what I know how to create, or what I'm going to build next.

Chapter Three

Workplace Fairness & Mental Health

Getting let go from Syncreon / DP World wasn't just losing a job. It was losing your sense of worth, your routine, your stability, and your faith in fairness itself. I've lived through that. I'm still living through it.

This section exists because silence protects employers, not employees. Speaking honestly about your own workplace experience — your own lived experience — is legally protected speech. And it matters. Especially when the employer is a global logistics giant that can afford to do better.

  • Engineers are not disposable. You cannot build complex systems with people and then treat them like replaceable cogs when it's convenient.
  • Mental health is workplace health. The stress of job insecurity, unclear expectations, and sudden termination is real and lasting.
  • Transparency matters. People deserve to know where they stand — not to be blindsided after delivering real work.
  • NDA ≠ silence about your experience. You can't share proprietary information. You can talk about what happened to you. I am.
  • Building is therapy. Channeling pain into creation is not denial — it's the most defiant form of recovery there is.

If you've been let go, discarded, made to feel worthless —
by Syncreon, DP World, or any company —
you are not alone, and you are not done.
Build anyway.

I'm a GCU student studying Elementary Education and Special Education now. I'm building a future where I help kids who learn differently — because the system that failed me doesn't get to define me, and DP World doesn't get to write my ending.

The company that let me go is worth over $20 billion. The engineer they let go built all of this — alone, for free, after they showed him the door. Make of that what you will.
