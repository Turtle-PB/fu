Chapter One

The Story

I'm Paul Adcock. I'm a software engineer — self-taught, relentless, and proud of every line of code I've written. I worked at Syncreon / DP World in logistics and supply chain technology. I showed up. I delivered. I built systems that actually worked.

And then they let me go.

"You can fire the person, but you can't fire what they built."

I won't name names. I won't fabricate. I don't need to. The truth is damning enough on its own. What I will say is this: I was an engineer who cared deeply about the work, who put in the hours, who solved real problems in a complex automotive logistics environment — and was shown the door anyway.

No explanation that made sense. No recognition of what was delivered. Just gone.

But here's what they didn't count on: I'm a builder. You can take away the desk, the badge, the title. You can't take away what's in my head, what I know how to create, or what I'm going to build next. This page is the proof.

Chapter Three

Workplace Fairness & Mental Health

Getting let go isn't just losing a job. It's losing your sense of worth, your routine, your stability, and sometimes your faith in fairness itself. I've lived through that. I'm still living through it.

This section exists because silence protects employers, not employees. Speaking honestly about workplace experiences — your own lived experience — is legally protected speech. And it matters.

  • Engineers are not disposable. You cannot build complex systems with people and then treat them like replaceable parts.
  • Mental health is workplace health. The stress of job insecurity, unclear expectations, and sudden termination is real and lasting.
  • Transparency matters. People deserve to know where they stand — not to be blindsided.
  • NDA does not mean silence about your own experience. You can't share proprietary info, but you can absolutely talk about what happened to you.
  • Building is therapy. Channeling pain into creation is not denial — it's the most defiant form of recovery.

If you've been let go, discarded, made to feel worthless —
you are not alone, and you are not done.
Build anyway.

I'm a GCU student studying Elementary Education and Special Education now. I'm building a future where I help kids who learn differently — because the system that failed me doesn't get to define me. And the company that let me go doesn't get to write my ending.
